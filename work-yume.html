Case Study: YUME Gardens of Tucson

Responsive Website Redesign

Serene Japanese Garden and Cultural Center gets a new website that reflects it's beauty.

UX/UI DESIGN

Yume Gardens Responsive Website Redesign

Project Overview

The YUME Gardens website, while functional, was not designed with the user in mind. In our opinion, the site was text heavy, with little typographic hierarchy, and did not address what we considered the users priorities: buying tickets, hours & location. We wanted to create a website that was not only accessable but beautiful as well.

The Solution:

A responsive website redesign with emphasis on simplified navigation and user first design.

Team Members:

Michelle K, Julia K, Carolina L, and Peggy P.

My Roles:

Heuristic evaluation, IA, style guide & Figma design system, high fidelity mobile wireframes.

Timeline:

3 Weeks

EMPATHY
Research

We began the redesign by going through the website page by page to really understand what the organization was about and how they serve the community. Competitor Analysis and Heuristic Evaluations were done. User surveys were created and posted on social media, and one-on-one user interviews conducted. Our key takeaways:

97.7% of respondents prefer to purchase tickets online before visiting an event or venue.

62.8% indicated that a website's appearance influences whether they will visit a local or not.

44.2% primarily want admission price. 20.9% primarily want to know the location. 20.9% want to see photos of what to expect.

Proto Persona

Guy Mann, 29
English Teacher, Single
Tucson, AZ

BIO

Guy is a high school English teacher. He suffers from anxiety, and while he loves teaching, job stress can make it worse.

He lived in Japan and taught English as a second language for 5 years. He fell in love with the culture and would like to have stayed longer, but had to come back for family reasons.

GOALS

Guy wants to maintain his Japanese language ability, deepen his connection to Japanese culture, and reconnect with nature. He finds being outdoors and disconnecting with technology helps with his stress and anxiety.

WANTS & NEEDS

He wants to find a way to manage his mental health as well as maintain his connection to Japan.



Poor Guy, he’s stuck in the Arizona desert, so far away from his beloved Japan. He suffers from anxiety and his job teaching high school English is stressing him out. He wants to find a way to decompress and manage his anxiety without medication or expensive therapy.

As a team, we wanted to help potential visitors like Guy, discover the beauty and tranquility of YUME Japanese Gardens. The gardens are, “the living expression of an ancient Japanese heritage, conceived with a unique vision and a sensitive healing purpose”.

For our user then, YUME Japanese Gardens is a win/win. He can continue his Japanese cultural education as well as find peace and serenity with the gardens, “Path to Emotional Healing Program”.

Problem Statement:

How might we help our Proto Persona to discover the Healing Path program at YUME Gardens and reconnect him with the Japanese culture he so admires?

IDEATION
Card Sorting and Site Map

We reimagined the top navigation menu and recontructed the website. The intention was to make it easier to buy tickets, view photos, and learn more about the "Path to Emotional Healing Program".

Prototyping

Now that we knew what pages and features to prioritize we set about mapping out the new website. We created and tested basic wireframes. Iterations were made as needed. Once we were happy with the basics we set about styling our pages.

UI Style Guide

I elaborated on our initial style tile to create a style guide and a complete design system in Figma. The design system allowed team members to design independently using standardized elements.

TESTING
A/B Testing and User Feedback

Conducting usability testing of our prototypes, we concluded our testers had no problems navigating to the “Donations” page, finding the “Path to Emotional Healing Program” or finding directions to the garden. So far the redesign was as success, except for a few things.

User Feedback:

  • It would be much less confusing to split the "About Us" section from the "Visit Us" page and create a seperate page for it.
  • A floating "Register" button on the "Path to Emotional Healing" page would keep the CTA front and center (literally)on a very text heavy page.
  • Questions as to why are there seperate "Getting Here & Parking" and Map & Location" pages as they really seem to address the same or similar problems.
THE SOLUTION
Iterated Clickable Prototypes

Tempted to make my subheading read "Final Clickable Prototypes", I'm aware that a design is never really "done", and there will always be something I can revisit. I guess I can say this is my Final Clickable Prototype...for now!

CONCLUSION

Like everything in life, working in a group has its positives and negatives. I loved that we were able crush our 3 week deadline, on the other hand it was a little chaotic as we raced the clock. Communication, like in any working or personal relationship is crucial.

I feel like we missed mark on a few things however. I believe we got off on the wrong foot by treating the gardens like a tourist attraction rather than a non-profit organization. It took some doing to refocus on the non-profit aspect of the Gardens. Because there were 4 of us all working on the wireframes, the mobile and desktop prototypes have some inconsistencies that should be addressed in the future.

If this were a real project I would suggest a companion app with an interactive map of the garden, access to a digital version of the “Path to Emotional Healing” Program, or maybe even an audio guided tour of the gardens that changes with the seasons.

All in all it was a great experience, especially in this era of Covid, to work together and collaborate with other aspiring designers. I think we all learned from one another - not just the nuts and bolts of website redesign, but we got to glimpse each other’s work flows and problem solving processes.
